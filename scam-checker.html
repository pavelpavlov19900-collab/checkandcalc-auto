Signals that often appear in scam websites

Scam and phishing websites change all the time, but many of them share similar patterns. This free scam checker looks at simple, transparent signals that you can also verify yourself:

  • Very long or messy domain names with many words glued together.
  • Multiple dashes and numbers in the domain, often used to imitate a brand name.
  • Unusual or very cheap TLDs (domain endings) that are frequently abused in spam campaigns.
  • Using a raw IP address instead of a normal domain like 123.45.67.89/login.
  • Year-like numbers inside the domain such as 2023, 2024, 2025, which scammers often use in disposable sites.

None of these factors alone automatically means “scam”. Legit websites can also use them. However, when you see several of them combined, and especially if the website is asking you to pay or log in, it’s a strong sign that you should slow down and double-check.

How to double-check if a website is legit

The scam website checker is a good first step, but you should always combine it with a few manual checks. Here is a simple checklist you can use:

  1. Search the brand name + “reviews” in Google. Look for independent review sites, not only testimonials on the website itself.
  2. Check the contact and company details. Legit businesses usually have a physical address, company registration info and multiple ways to contact support.
  3. Compare prices with other stores. If the price is dramatically cheaper than everywhere else, it can be a sign of a fake online shop.
  4. Look at the payment methods. Only bank transfer or crypto with no buyer protection is a red flag. Reputable stores often support cards or trusted payment providers.
  5. Trust your intuition. If something feels off – strange language, rushed countdown timers, aggressive pop-ups – it’s better to walk away.

FAQ: common questions about the scam checker

Is this scam website checker 100% accurate?

No. This tool is a heuristic checker, not a replacement for professional security tools or your own judgement. It looks at obvious domain patterns and returns a simple low, medium or high risk level. A low risk score does not guarantee that a website is safe, and a high risk score does not guarantee that it is a scam – it only shows that the domain name looks suspicious.

Can a legit website still get a high risk score?

Yes, it is possible. For example, a small personal project or a new online shop might use a long domain with numbers or a cheap TLD to save money. That can trigger several of the rules and result in a higher score even if the website is honest. Always combine the result with real-world checks such as company registration, external reviews and common sense.

Can I use this tool before I click on random links?

Yes. If you receive a strange link in email, messaging apps or social media, you can paste it here first to see how risky it looks. If the score is medium or high and something feels off, do not log in or pay on that website. Contact the company through official channels instead.
